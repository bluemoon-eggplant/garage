## Background

I had set the fuel mixture rich so that it would start on the first try even in winter without using the choke.  
However, restarting after running would sometimes be difficult.  
In particular, starting in the scorching summer heat of the garage was quite poor.  
So, for the first time since doing a carburetor overhaul a year ago, I decided to change the settings.

## The Problem

A problem I had noticed even during the carburetor overhaul, but I confirmed again that I couldn't properly adjust the pilot screw that controls idling.  
Apparently, adjusting the pilot screw should change the idle RPM, and you determine the screw opening based on those changes up and down, but no matter how much I adjust it, the idle remains unchanged.  
I suspect that since I installed a brand new float as-is, the fuel level is too high, and so much fuel is being supplied that the pilot screw adjustment has no effect.  
However, adjusting this is difficult because keeping the bike level is challenging, and more importantly, adjusting the float with the bike installed is too much work, so if I do it, I want to remove the carburetor first.  
At that time, I also want to service the fuel petcock area on the tank. (It seems like gasoline is seeping out somehow)

## Temporary Settings for Now

Synchronization is fine, not out of balance.

<Image {...IMAGE_SIZES.FIXED.MDX_LG} src={Img1} alt="Checking carburetor synchronization with a vacuum gauge" />

The idle was a bit low, so I adjusted it to 1,300 RPM as specified in the service manual.

<Image {...IMAGE_SIZES.FIXED.MDX_LG} src={Img2} alt="Checking idle RPM" />

As for the all-important pilot screw:

* Fully closed: Engine won't start
* 1.25 turns out: Engine won't start, but there is an initial pop

Based on this, and judging that the service manual spec of 1.5 turns out is too rich, I decided to split the difference and try 1.375 turns out to see how it goes.  
The goal is one-touch starting anytime! But I wonder how close I can get to that.
