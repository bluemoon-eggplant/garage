## Why I Bought It

After buying the ZZR1400, its poor maneuverability made me want a smaller-displacement bike, and even back then I had my mind set on a Renaissa in orange.
However, since I don't ride motorcycles that often, the need never justified itself and time just passed.
Later, after moving, I got a garage, which gave me not just parking space but room to work on bikes.
That rekindled my desire to buy a Renaissa as my first practice machine for wrenching.
But it's a discontinued and unpopular model, so available units are scarce -- especially in orange.
I reserved one on Goo Bike and went to see the bike in person.
The unit I saw had a cracked headlight housing, the exterior wasn't in great shape, and it felt overpriced for what it was.
On top of that, the shop staff's attitude was underwhelming, so I didn't pull the trigger.
That's when I decided that if I was going to work on it myself anyway, buying off Yahoo Auctions would be fine -- and committed to buying a motorcycle through auction for the first time.

## How I Bought It

I started searching on Yahoo Auctions and quickly found my target.
It was nearly stock, with a Super Trapp exhaust installed but the original exhaust in good condition included -- a dream come true for a stock purist.
I contacted the seller and drove over 400km one way to inspect the bike.
The condition was decent. Apparently the previous owner had set it up for his daughter, but she couldn't handle the seat height and stopped riding, so he was letting it go.
He'd done some restoration work -- fuel hoses and the petcock had been replaced, and tank rust didn't seem to be an issue.
I decided to buy it.
After winning a bidding war, I successfully secured it.

## Delivery Day

Having won the auction, the bike was over 400km away.
Since it seemed to be running well when I inspected it, I decided to ride it home.
The plan was: park my car near a local station, pick up the bike, ride home, then take the bullet train back to pick up my car and the stock exhaust -- a two-round-trip operation.
Well, a problem became apparent immediately after picking up the bike.
The front brake was barely functional -- even squeezing the lever with full force only produced minimal stopping power.
I essentially rode home using almost only the rear brake.
I'd been skeptical about the rear drum brake's performance, but it was surprisingly effective.
The engine was running fine, and even though it was my first carburetor-equipped bike, it handled the elevation changes without issue and I made it home safely.
However, I did run out of gas along the way and barely made it to a station on reserve.
I'd trusted the online claims of ~35km/L fuel economy, and that overconfidence was my punishment.

## Review

### Exterior

I think the Renaissa's best angle is the tail.
I generally like round-headlight classic bikes like the SR400, but many of them have tail sections I'm not fond of.
The SR's seat is too long, giving it a stretched-out look, and Honda's classics have a sort of stubby feel I don't care for.
I prefer a tail that tapers off gradually, and the Renaissa delivers exactly that.
The fact that the headlight housing is body-colored -- unusual for most bikes -- is another design element I appreciate.
Also, because of the V-twin engine, the body is dramatically narrow, and from the side, the engine's presence really stands out. That's a unique trait I love.
V-twin naked bikes are rare, so the side profile has a presence that belies its 250cc displacement.

### Riding Position & Handling

Despite having bar handlebars, it demands a slightly forward lean, which -- is that supposed to make it a sport bike?
I wouldn't call it a comfortable position, honestly.
Also, perhaps because design was prioritized, there's not much room for knee grip -- I wouldn't say it's impossible, but it's not easy.
This makes it hard to settle into a consistent seating position, which contributes to fatigue on longer rides.
Both feet reach the ground flat, but at 179cm, I might be too tall for this bike -- my lower body feels a bit cramped.
That said, the bike weighs only about 150kg, so handling at low speeds is a breeze.
Combined with the narrow body, you can hop on it like a scooter, which makes it very practical as a daily rider.

### Riding Performance

V-twins are supposedly all about their distinctive pulse, but honestly, at 250cc, there's no real drama.
What did surprise me was how the low-RPM throb smooths out at higher revs into a vibration-free, almost parallel-twin-like character.
I once rented a Honda Steed, and despite being a 400cc, I remember being disappointed that it didn't go fast no matter how much I revved it. The Renaissa actually feels faster.
27hp was reasonably good even for its era, and it shows.
On top of that, the solid low-end torque means it almost never stalls.
Perhaps that's a benefit of the long-stroke engine originally designed for the Virago.
In corners, the lightweight body lets you lean it over dramatically, really highlighting how reluctant the ZZR1400 is to turn by comparison.
However, maybe due to the narrow tires, the rear slipped in a corner during winter, and I got a taste of the challenges that come with a lightweight bike.

## Modifications

The focus is on keeping things as close to stock as possible while maintaining a clean appearance.

* Replaced the rear shocks with OEM SR400 units
* Replaced the mirrors with OEM SR400 mirrors
* Installed an SRV250 fuel petcock so fuel flow can be shut off when removing the tank

## Troubles

I bought it with the intention of doing my own maintenance, but this being a private sale, there were plenty of issues.
Also, being my first time wrenching, I made a lot of mistakes.

* Front fork oil leak (overhauled, inner tubes replaced)
* Front brake barely working (old and low fluid -- overhauled)
* Poor starting, stalling while riding (carburetor overhauled -- needle jet was set to a non-stock spec)
* Oil drain bolt stripped
* Crankcase cover bolt installed incorrectly, threads stripped
* Front brake caliper support bolt stripped
