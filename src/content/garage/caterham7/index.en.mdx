## Why I Bought It

Up until then, the RX-7 was the only fast car I'd ever driven, but there was one car that absolutely floored me.  
That was my friend's CLA45AMG.  
It was mind-blowing.  
The power and response were enough to make me disillusioned with the RX-7 I had loved so much.  
Realizing I'd carry this inferiority complex with me unless I did something, I urgently started searching for a car that could deliver more excitement than the CLA45AMG.  
Since I loved the RX-7's concept, what I should pursue was lightness -- and it didn't take long to arrive at the "Super Seven."

## How I Bought It

If you look at CarSensor and such, you'll notice that these cars are overwhelmingly scarce on the market.  
So, factoring in maintenance, I started with finding a shop first.  
I ended up contacting the second shop, where I told them I'd buy if something matching the following criteria appeared at under 5 million yen:

* Fuel-injected (carburetor seemed too hard to maintain)
* Around 1.6L displacement (the kei-car engine model's tires were too narrow for my liking)
* Full green repaint (my dream of British Racing Green)

Honestly, these were uncompromising conditions, so I wasn't expecting much and was prepared to wait. But about 3 months later, a matching car turned up.  
The budget was blown, of course, but it came with a spare tire rack, wire throttle (newer models have electronic throttle, which doesn't have the best reputation), and low mileage -- a pretty solid car overall, so I pulled the trigger.  
After purchase, there were various parts that needed replacing, and the high-mount stop lamp was missing so it couldn't pass inspection, which delayed delivery.

It was a mid-winter delivery, and I remember it being a clear, perfect day for open-top driving.  
The shop wasn't that far from home, but I took the highway to try it out.  
And I was terrified by the incredibly quick steering response.  
The slightest road imperfection would yank the steering wheel, so you have to keep a firm grip at all times.
I didn't get to experience the cornering performance that day, but between my first convertible experience and the sheer satisfaction of driving such an unusual machine, it was a fulfilling delivery day.

## Review

### Exterior

The body shape, clearly unlike any modern car, has a unique charm that's neither conventionally cool nor cute.
Later-model Sevens have LED lighting, smaller headlights, and modern turn signal units that don't match the car's character, but my Seven has all-bulb lighting.
This means the headlights are large, which emphasizes the car's cute and classic vibe.  
I also love the generous use of chrome on the headlight housings, mirrors, wipers, and other parts.  
My Seven is unusual in that the exhaust exits from the rear -- most Sevens have side-exit exhausts, so this is a distinctive touch.  
While this car has a sporty concept, the classic elements I mentioned combined with the spare tire rack give it more of a touring feel.  
  
### Interior

It's hard to even call it an "interior," so it's tough to evaluate, but the seating position is surprisingly comfortable.
Perhaps because you sit with your legs stretched out, even without reclining seats, long-distance drives aren't a problem.
This is a matter of preference, but all the gauges are black-faced, and as a white-gauge purist, that's my only complaint.  
  
### Driving Performance

It was more than enough to cure the inferiority complex triggered by the CLA45AMG.
The cornering performance that comes from sheer lightness is on another level -- even the RX-7, known as a cornering machine, feels heavy by comparison.
The downhill speed in particular is otherworldly -- there's a fear and thrill unique to this car.  
With more weight on the rear, the front end turns in beautifully, and the wheelbase is surprisingly long for the body size, so it's not twitchy.  
The cherry on top is the synergy between the tube-frame rigidity and Bilstein suspension.  
Despite being so light, the car doesn't get unsettled even on bumpy surfaces.  
The suspension in particular was awe-inspiring -- for a car this low, the ride quality is remarkably good.  
On top of that, it's not harsh, yet there's virtually no body roll in corners. The low center of gravity helps too, of course.  
It made me think, "my next set of dampers on any car will be Bilstein, no question" -- that's how much of a fan I became.  
After the flywheel was lightened, the car felt completely different. The ultra-responsive engine let me wring it to high RPMs, and I could enjoy full-adrenaline driving.  
I'd never given much thought to turbo vs. NA preference, but this car instantly converted me to team NA -- that's how intoxicating the experience is.  
  
### Issues

British car quality is low -- well, terrible, to be frank.
For a simple car with fewer parts, it breaks down constantly.
There's awful rattling from the interior panels and exhaust mounts, and the overall fit and finish is the worst.  
The speedometer needle moves so erratically you'd think it's from a WWII fighter plane.  
Also, due to the car's construction, stone chips are unavoidable.  
I'm not the type to fuss over it, but given that recent Sevens have shifted toward higher quality, stone chips would probably be a real concern for those owners.  
Moving to driving-related issues, the shift feel is awful.  
It's basically "shove a stick (with an ultra-short throw) into the shift gate with brute force!" -- end of story. There's zero satisfaction in it.  
After driving a Roadster with its close-ratio gears, I really wish the Seven had a closer-ratio box too.  
The ratios feel a bit too long.  
  
## Modifications

Performance-wise, the flywheel lightening is about it.
For exterior and interior:

* Swapped the spare tire on the rear rack for a leather trunk
* Upgraded to chrome wipers
* Replaced the license plate light cover with a chrome one
* Installed gauge bezels
* Installed a front dashcam
